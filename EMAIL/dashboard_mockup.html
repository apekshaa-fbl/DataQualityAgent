Quality Metrics — Definitions & How They Are Calculated
📊 Coverage
Coverage % = (Records with Email / Total Records) × 100
Measures how many company–country records have at least one email — either in PRIMARY_EMAIL or OTHER_EMAILS. A record counts as covered even if the email is a gmail or generic address. Coverage only measures presence, not quality.
Current: 42.3%  ·  Target: 70%
🎯 Accuracy
Accuracy % = weighted composite score per record, averaged across all records with email
Not a single check — accuracy is a weighted composite of 5 signals per email record:

40% Domain match — email root domain = company FQDN root
20% Not a cross-company duplicate
20% Not a blocked/noreply address
15% Not a free provider (for companies with 50+ employees)
5% Valid email format

Each record scores 0–100 on these signals combined. Country Accuracy = average across all records with email in that country.
Current: 61.8%  ·  Target: 80%
✅ Consistency
Consistency % = (Valid-format, Non-blocked Emails / Records with Email) × 100
Measures whether emails are structurally usable. An email is consistent if it: (1) passes format validation — correct regex, has @, valid TLD — and (2) is not a blocked no-reply address. Free provider and generic emails are not penalised here — they are flagged separately as signals.

Scoring Rules — How Every Email Gets a Priority Score
Priority Score per Email  Max possible: 9 points
Each signal is checked independently. Points add up. Higher score = better email = ranked first.
+3
Domain matches company FQDN (strongest signal)
The root of the email's domain equals the company's FQDN root.
acme.com → email info@acme.com ✅ matches (root: acme = acme)
acme.com → email info@mail.acme.com ✅ also matches (root: acme = acme)
acme.com → email info@gmail.com ❌ does not match (root: gmail ≠ acme)
Worth 3 points because this is the strongest proof the email truly belongs to this company.
+2
Not a free provider domain
Email domain is not on the free-provider blocklist (gmail.com, yahoo.com, hotmail.com, outlook.com, icloud.com, live.com, etc.).
Worth 2 points because a company-domain email is far more trustworthy than a personal inbox. Exception: micro businesses (<10 emp) get a +1 bonus even when they use gmail — see below.
+2
Not a blocked (no-reply) address
Email local part is not noreply, no-reply, do-not-reply or donotreply.
Worth 2 points because blocked addresses cannot receive replies — they are completely unusable for any outreach purpose. Even if domain-matched, a noreply address ranks below a generic info@ address.
+1
Valid email format
Passes regex: must contain @, have a valid local part (letters/numbers/dots/underscores), and a TLD of 2+ characters. An invalid format email is structurally broken — no tool can send to it. Worth 1 point as a baseline hygiene check.
+1
Not a cross-company duplicate
This email does not appear on more than one distinct FIRMABLE_ID. If the same email (e.g. info@shared.com) is assigned to 50 different companies, it is almost certainly a data error. Worth 1 point — deprioritised vs a unique email but not excluded entirely.
+1 bonus
Free provider + micro company (<10 global employees)
Exception to the free-provider penalty. If a company has fewer than 10 employees globally (GLOBAL_EMP_COUNT), a gmail or yahoo email is often their only real business contact — do not penalise unfairly. The +1 bonus partially offsets the −2 for being a free provider, giving a net score of 3 instead of 2.
Selection Process — Step by Step
Applied per company per region (ID + REGION_CODE)
1Collect all emails — combine PRIMARY_EMAIL with each value in OTHER_EMAILS for that company record.
2Score each email — apply all 6 rules above. Each email gets a score from 0 to 9.
3Rank by score descending — ties are broken by: domain-matched first, then generic role-based (info@, contact@), then free provider, then blocked.
4Select top 5 — take the 5 highest-ranked emails. If the company has fewer than 5, take all available. Leave remaining slots empty (null) — do not pad with lower-quality emails.
5Store with scores — save EMAIL_1 through EMAIL_5 alongside SCORE_1 through SCORE_5. The average score across filled slots becomes the company's email quality rating.
Why top 5? Most companies need 1–3 contact emails for outreach. Capping at 5 prevents noise from companies with dozens of low-quality addresses, while still providing backup options if the primary bounces.
